Welcome to the
Kamogelo Project

In Setswana, one of the native languages of Botswana, kamogelo means “to welcome”. Here at the Kamogelo Orphan and Vulnerable Children Project, in Mogoditshane, Botswana, we are committed to doing just that. Welcoming children who often have nowhere else to go.

ABOUT US

As of this year, the Kameglo Project is taking care of over 90 children ranging from 21 months to 6 years of age. This number continues to increase as we strive to never have to turn away a child in need. Our mission is to provide a stigma-free environment for children deeply effected by the devastating and recent HIV/AIDS epidemic, where they can grow intellectually, physically, and emotionally all at no coast to the children or their families. We offer a wide range of services including education, extracurricular activities, skills training, food, clothing, transportation, and increased access to medical care. We recognize that addressing the complex needs of orphans and vulnerable children is a community effort.


The story the story of the Kamogelo Project began long before the construction of our current building in 2005. In the wake of the HIV/AIDS epidemic in the mid 1990s, our sponsoring organization, the Tirisanyo Catholic Commission started home-based care to help the epidemic’s many victims. At the height of the epidemic, Botswana was hit particularly hard when many community members succumbed to the disease. As aid workers traveled from house to house they realized that the parents were usually too ill to properly take care of their children. Furthermore most of the homes in the area are only one room, the children were forced to watch their parent’s condition deteriorate all well being denied the proper education that young children need.

The Tirisanyo Catholic Commission realized something needed to be done quickly. They set up a day care and school in a local 2-room church that had originally been a one family house. All the students activities were conducted outside due to limited capacity. Fortunately, the Rotary Club of Gaborone stepped into help. With generous donations from the First National Bank of Botswana (FNB), the United States Department of Defense, the U.S. European Command, the U.S. Embassy Botswana, and the People of the United States of America to construct a new and expansive set of buildings for the school in 2005. After the groundbreaking, the Inner Wheel Club of Gaborone donated furniture and other goods to help stock the school.

Volunteer With Us

Every year since the founding of the Kamogelo Daycare Centre, we welcome volunteers from across the globe through our Volunteer Program. Previous volunteers include university students from Germany, USA, Sweden, Pakistan, Canada as well as numerous local volunteers.
In addition to our own program, the centre welcomes two volunteer interns from University of Pennsylvania through the Botswana – UPenn Partnership (USA) during the months of June, July and August each year. A group of nursing students from the same partnership perform annual examinations on all our children.

Basic daily needs

We offer a wide range of services including education, extracurricular activities, skills training, food, clothing, transportation, and increased access to medical care. We recognize that addressing the complex needs of orphans and vulnerable children is a community effort. Our children come to the Kamogelo Project’s school from 8 am to 1 pm every weekday. Our children start the day with a morning prayer that is followed by a healthy and warm breakfast. Following breakfast, classes and playtime persist until 12:30 when the children break for lunch before returning home. Only 5% of our children come from a home with a caregiver with stable work or income. It becomes essential to help feed these children because the 2 hot meals they receive here are often their only consistent meals. Families also struggle to clothe these children. Many only have the uniform we try distribute annually funds pending. Similarly the shoes we aim to distribute, when funds allow, are usually the child’s only shoes for the year.

Due to the fact that some of the children travel up to 20 km (12 miles) in a single day in coming to school, we offer transportation for many of our commuting students. We operate one combi-sized car (a van-like vehicle that seats about 15 adults, typical the region.) Unfortunately, we only have enough funds to employ one driver forcing our combi to make multiple trips both before and after classes. Steadily rising fuel costs make offering this service one of our largest financial burdens.

We hope to continue to sustain our current offerings and be able to expand both our services and the amount of children that we can accommodate given the HIV/AIDS epidemic has effected so many families in our village and the surrounding areas.
